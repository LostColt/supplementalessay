Here's what schools are really asking with this one: Who are you, and how will you contribute to our campus?

They're not just collecting diversity statistics. They want to know if you understand yourself, if you can reflect on your experiences, and if you'll actually do something when you get to campus.

If you grew up in a suburb with a stable family and good schools, you still have communities you belong to and ways you've contributed. Maybe it's your debate team, your church youth group, your neighborhood friend group, your family's cultural traditions, your online gaming community. Community doesn't have to mean struggle. It just has to be real.

Brainstorming Your Content

1 / 6

The Two Parts You Need

Part 1: The Community Itself

Describe it with enough detail that someone who's never been there can picture it. Not just "my family" but the specific dynamics, traditions, values.

Part 2: Your Role In It

This is the key part. What do you actually do there? How do you contribute? Don't just say "I'm a leader." Show it through a specific moment. Maybe you're the person who remembers everyone's birthday, or the one who asks the quiet kid for their opinion.

How to Open Strong

❌ The Snooze Button (Generic)

"My family is important to me because they have taught me many valuable lessons about life."

✅ The Hook (Specific)

"Every Sunday, my abuela's kitchen fills with the smell of sofrito, and that's when the real conversations start-the ones where my cousins and I switch between English and Spanish mid-sentence and nobody thinks twice about it."

Connecting Your Role to College

Don't just describe your community and stop there. Connect it to what you'll do in college. Be specific-mention actual clubs, organizations, or ways you'll contribute.

❌ Wrong Way

"This experience taught me the importance of community, and I plan to get involved in college."

✅ Right Way

"At USC, I want to join the Filipino Student Association not just to find that same mix of cultural pride and inside jokes, but to help organize the kind of food-centered gatherings that always brought my family together-the ones where real conversations happen around the table."
